# Mount Shasta — Deep Dives: Boots, Snow Camping, Waste Management

---

## 1. Ski Boot Strategy: Wear the Whole Time vs. Carry & Switch

This is one of the biggest decisions on a ski mountaineering trip and there's no single right answer. It depends on your boots, your pain tolerance, and how much weight you want to carry.

### Option A: Wear AT Boots the Entire Trip ✅ RECOMMENDED

**The case for it:**
- Simplest logistics. One pair of footwear. No transitions. No extra weight.
- Modern AT boots (Scarpa Maestrale, Tecnica Zero G, Dynafit Hoji) have genuine walk modes with 50–70° range of motion. They're designed to tour all day.
- You eliminate 5–7 lbs of dead weight (approach shoes + the boot swap itself).
- No risk of cold, wet feet during a mid-climb boot swap at 12,000 ft in the dark.
- Crampons stay fitted to one pair of boots — no compatibility surprises.

**The downsides:**
- AT boots are stiffer than hiking boots. The approach through the forest (Bunny Flat → Horse Camp, ~1.8 mi) is less comfortable.
- Bootpacking above Red Banks in ski boots is clunkier than in dedicated mountaineering boots. The flex and ankle support are different.
- If your AT boots run cold, your feet may suffer during the 1 AM–5 AM bootpack before the sun comes up.

**How to make it work:**
- **Break in your boots before the trip.** Seriously. Skin 3–4 times in them before Shasta. Know your hot spots.
- **Use the walk mode liberally.** Lock it to ski mode only when actually skiing.
- **Thick ski socks + toe warmers** for the pre-dawn bootpack. Chemical warmers inside AT boots make a massive difference.
- **Loosen buckles at camp.** Don't walk around camp in fully buckled ski boots. Pop the top buckles, open the walk mode, let your feet breathe.
- **Liner management:** Pull liners out at camp and sleep with them in your sleeping bag. Cold, damp liners in the morning = misery. Warm, dry liners = happy feet all day.

### Option B: Carry Approach Shoes, Switch at Camp / Ski Stash

**The case for it:**
- Genuine comfort on the forest approach and around camp.
- Light trail runners (8 oz) are nothing in the pack.
- Some people bootpack better in mountaineering boots than AT boots — more ankle support, warmer, stiffer sole for kicking steps.

**The downsides:**
- Extra weight. Trail runners + mountaineering boots + AT boots = three pairs of footwear. That's absurd for most people.
- If you're only carrying approach shoes (not mountaineering boots), you still bootpack in AT boots above the ski stash. So what did you gain?
- Boot transitions in the cold and dark are fumbling, annoying, and slow. Laces freeze. Buckles ice up. You're trying to swap footwear at 1 AM on a snow field.

**The realistic version:**
- Wear AT boots for the entire climb and descent.
- Bring ultralight camp shoes (down booties or foam sandals, ~4 oz) for comfort at Helen Lake camp. That's it.
- Don't carry approach shoes for the trail. It's 1.8 miles of forest. Your AT boots in walk mode are fine.

### Option C: Two-Boot System (AT Boots + Mountaineering Boots)

**Only makes sense if:**
- Your AT boots are genuinely bad for bootpacking (too soft, too cold, crampon compatibility issues)
- You're okay carrying 6+ extra pounds
- You have a clear transition point (Red Banks ski stash)

**How it would work:**
1. Skin to Helen Lake in AT boots
2. At camp: stash AT boots + skis
3. Summit push in mountaineering boots + crampons
4. Descend to Helen Lake, switch back to AT boots
5. Ski down

**Verdict:** Not worth it for most people. Modern AT boots with walk mode and good crampons handle the full trip. Save the weight for food and water.

### Bottom Line

| Scenario | Recommendation |
|----------|---------------|
| Modern AT boots with walk mode (Scarpa Maestrale RS, Tecnica Zero G Tour Pro, etc.) | Wear them the whole time. Bring down booties for camp. |
| Older/stiffer AT boots without good walk mode | Consider light approach shoes for forest + camp only |
| Very cold feet, bad circulation | Add chemical toe warmers. Still wear AT boots. Don't add a second boot. |
| Multi-day base camp with rest day | Camp shoes make more sense here. But for a 2-day Shasta trip, skip it. |

### Camp Footwear: The One Exception

Bring **down booties or insulated camp shoes** (~4 oz). After skinning all day, your feet need a break. These are:
- Worn only at Helen Lake camp
- Stuffed in sleeping bag overnight to keep warm
- Not for walking on snow outside camp (no grip, no insulation from ground)
- Absolute luxury item that weighs nothing

Good options: Baffin Cush, Outdoor Research Tundra Aerogel, REI Co-op Down Booties, or even neoprene socks with a plastic bag over them in a pinch.

## 2. Snow Camping at Helen Lake — The Full Breakdown

If you've never snow camped, this section is critical. It's a fundamentally different experience from dirt camping. Everything is colder, wetter, and more deliberate.

### Site Selection

**Where to camp at Helen Lake (~10,443 ft):**
- Look for a relatively flat bench on the south or southeast side of the lake area
- Wind protection is priority #1. Look for natural terrain features — ridges, rock outcrops, depressions — that block the prevailing wind (typically from the west/northwest)
- Avoid camping directly in Avalanche Gulch drainage — you want to be off to the side, not in the path of anything sliding from above
- Don't camp on a convexity (wind-loaded, avalanche-prone). Camp on a flat or slight depression.
- Check uphill: is there steep terrain above you that could slide? Move to the side.

### Building Your Tent Platform

1. **Stomp out a flat platform** with your skis on. Walk back and forth across an area ~10 ft × 10 ft. Pack the snow as hard as you can.
2. **Let it set for 20–30 minutes** (sintering). Stomped snow re-bonds and hardens. This is critical — skip this and your platform will be lumpy mush all night.
3. **Stomp it again.** Then set your tent.
4. **Make it level.** Use a shovel to shave high spots. A tilted platform means you slide into your tent-mate all night.
5. **Orient the tent with the narrow end into the wind.** Less surface area for gusts to catch.

### Anchoring the Tent

Regular tent stakes are useless in snow. Options:

| Method | How | Notes |
|--------|-----|-------|
| **Snow stakes** | Purpose-built aluminum stakes, 10–12 inches. Bury horizontally, pack snow on top. | Best option. Light. Cheap. Buy a set of 6. |
| **Deadman anchors** | Bury a stuff sack full of snow, tie guyline to it, pack snow on top. | Free — use your extra stuff sacks. |
| **Ski anchors** | Bury skis vertically in snow as anchors for guylines. | Good for the 2 main windward guylines. |
| **T-slot** | Cut a horizontal trench in snow, lay the stake/object in it perpendicular to the pull direction, pack snow on top. | Most secure. Takes time. Do this for critical guylines. |

**Pack snow firmly over every anchor.** Let it set 10 min. Test each guyline before you trust it.

### Keeping Warm

The ground (snow) is your biggest enemy. It's 32°F max and will suck heat from your body all night.

- **Sleeping pad: R-value 5 or higher.** This is non-negotiable. Two pads stacked (closed-cell foam on bottom + inflatable on top) is even better. Your pad matters more than your sleeping bag.
- **Sleeping bag: rated 10–15°F.** Down is lighter but worthless if wet. Synthetic is heavier but insulates when damp. Your call. Keep down bags in a dry bag / waterproof stuff sack.
- **Sleep in dry base layers.** Change out of your sweaty climbing clothes before bed. Damp clothes = cold night.
- **Hot water bottle.** Boil water, pour into a Nalgene, screw tight, put it in your sleeping bag 10 min before bed. Game-changer.
- **Eat a calorie-dense snack before bed.** Your body generates heat by digesting food. Peanut butter, cheese, chocolate.
- **Pee before bed.** Your body wastes energy keeping urine warm. (See the pee bottle discussion in Section 3.)

### Water Management

- **All water comes from melting snow.** There's no liquid water at Helen Lake.
- **Melt efficiency:** Fill the pot with a small amount of water first (seed water), then add snow gradually. Dumping a pot full of dry snow on a flame is slow and can scorch your pot.
- **Melt enough before bed.** You need at least 1L per person for the overnight + summit departure. Melting snow at midnight when you want to leave is not fun.
- **Keep water from freezing:** Sleep with your Nalgenes inside your sleeping bag (between your legs or along your torso). Water left outside will freeze solid by 2 AM.
- **Stove placement:** Use a piece of foam, plywood, or a flat rock under your stove. Stoves on snow melt a hole and tip over. If using a canister stove, keep the canister warm (body heat or lukewarm water bath) — propane/butane mix performs badly below 20°F.

### Kitchen Area

- **Dig a kitchen trench.** A 3-foot-deep hole creates a windbreak and a seat (sit on the lip, feet in the trench). Flat shelf for the stove.
- **Keep the kitchen downwind from the tent.** Spilled fuel + nylon tent = fire.
- **Boil water for dinner, breakfast, AND pre-fill water bottles for summit** all in one session. Minimize stove time.

### Other Snow Camp Tips

- **Dig a vestibule trench** in front of your tent door — step down into it to enter/exit without tracking snow into the tent.
- **Keep boots in the tent vestibule**, not outside. Boots left on the snow will freeze solid. If using AT boots, loosen all buckles and open the shell. Pull liners and sleep with them.
- **Condensation management:** Your breath freezes on the tent walls and creates frost. It will snow on you. Vent the tent (crack a door, open vents) even if it's cold. A slightly colder but dry tent beats a warm, dripping one.
- **Mark your camp.** When you leave at 1 AM in the dark, everything looks the same. Stick a ski pole with a blinky light or reflective tape outside your tent so you can find it on the way back down.
- **Sunscreen your tent.** In mid-May, direct sun melts snow FAST. Your tent can sink into a melt pool by mid-morning. If you leave camp set up for summit day, know that your platform will soften. Not a disaster, just annoying.

### Snow Camping Gear Checklist (Beyond Main Gear List)

- [ ] Snow shovel (you're carrying one for avy anyway — dual purpose)
- [ ] Snow stakes (6–8) or deadman anchor materials
- [ ] Extra closed-cell foam pad (sit pad doubles as stove base)
- [ ] Whisk broom or small brush (clear snow from tent)
- [ ] Repair kit: tent pole splint, duct tape, seam grip
- [ ] Candle lantern or ultralight LED (morale in the tent)

## 3. Human Waste Management: The Practical Guide

This is the part nobody wants to talk about but everybody has to deal with. On Mount Shasta, **packing out human waste is mandatory above treeline.** Rangers enforce it. It's required by the Forest Service. And it's the right thing to do — Shasta gets 20,000+ climbers per year. If everyone dug cat holes, the mountain would be a sewer.

### What Are WAG Bags?

WAG = Waste Aggregation and Gelling. A WAG bag is a double-layered bag system:
- **Inner bag:** Contains a gelling powder (originally developed by NASA) that solidifies liquid on contact, neutralizes odor, and reduces pathogens.
- **Outer bag:** Puncture-resistant zip-seal bag that creates a secondary containment layer.

When you go, the gel does its work in seconds. You seal it up. It doesn't smell (much). It's compact. You carry it out and throw it in a regular trash can.

### Where to Get Them

- **FREE at the Mount Shasta Ranger District** (204 W Alma St) and at the Bunny Flat trailhead kiosk
- Also sold at The Fifth Season and outdoor stores
- Brand names: Cleanwaste WAG BAG, Restop RS2, Biffy Bag
- **Bring 2–3 per person per day.** For a 2-day trip, that's 4–6 bags per person. Grab extras.

### How to Use Them — Step by Step

**Setup:**
1. Find a spot away from camp, tent, and kitchen — at least 50 ft downwind.
2. Dig a small trench or flat spot in the snow for stability (you'll be squatting).
3. Open the outer bag. Remove the inner bag with the gelling powder.
4. Open the inner bag and unfold it. The opening is wide — it's designed for this.

**The Act:**
5. Squat over the inner bag. Hold it in position or set it in the snow trench.
6. Do your thing. Urine goes in too — the gelling powder handles both.
7. Toilet paper goes IN the bag. Don't bury TP separately.

**Seal and Store:**
8. Press out excess air from the inner bag. Tie or seal it shut.
9. Place the sealed inner bag into the outer zip-seal bag. Squeeze out air. Seal it.
10. You now have a compact, double-sealed, odor-controlled package.

**Storage on the mountain:**
11. Put the sealed WAG bag in a dedicated stuff sack or dry bag that you use ONLY for waste. Label it. Don't mix it with your food bag.
12. Clip the waste bag to the OUTSIDE of your pack if possible, or store in a dedicated pack pocket.
13. At the car, throw it in a regular trash can. WAG bags are landfill-safe.

### The Nuances Nobody Tells You

**Timing your #2:**
- Best times: at camp before bed, or at camp before the summit push. You're near your supplies, you can set up properly, and you're not fumbling with bags on a 35° slope.
- Worst time: mid-climb at 12,000 ft in the dark with wind blowing. If you can manage your schedule (caffeine timing, food choices), try to go at camp.
- That said — when you gotta go, you gotta go. Have a WAG bag accessible in your summit pack, not buried at the bottom.

**The pee situation (separate from WAG bags):**

Urinating is more frequent at altitude (your body dumps fluid as part of acclimatization) and much more annoying in a sleeping bag at 2 AM.

| Method | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|
| **Pee bottle** (dedicated wide-mouth Nalgene) | Never leave the tent. Fast. Warm. | Must be wide-mouth. Must be clearly labeled. MUST NOT be confused with drinking water. Disastrous if knocked over. |
| **Pee outside** | Simple. No bottle management. | You have to get dressed, unzip tent, put on boots, walk out. At 1 AM on a snow field, this takes 10 min and you fully wake up. |
| **Pee funnel (for all genders)** | Makes pee bottle viable for everyone. | Practice at home first. Seriously. |

**Pee bottle protocol:**
- Use a 32 oz wide-mouth Nalgene with a VERY different look from your water bottle. Different color. Duct tape wrapped around it. Sharpie "PEE" on it.
- Keep it in the sleeping bag vestibule area.
- For men: works in lying position with practice. Open bag vent for clearance.
- For women: a pee funnel (Freshette, pStyle, or Shewee) is almost mandatory. Practice at home. It works, but not on the first try.
- Empty it outside in the morning away from camp and the route. Urine on snow = yellow stain + smell for every climber behind you.

**Managing used WAG bags during the trip:**

- Carry a thick, opaque **dry bag (8–10L)** designated as your "waste kit." This holds:
  - Unused WAG bags
  - Used/sealed WAG bags
  - Hand sanitizer
  - TP (in a zip-lock to keep dry)
  - Small bottle of hand soap
  - A few baby wipes (pack them out — don't bury)
- The gelling agent in WAG bags does neutralize odor, but on a warm May afternoon with sun hitting your pack, double-bagging is your friend.
- **Never leave used WAG bags at camp** when you leave for summit. Animals and wind. Take them with you or bury the waste bag under snow (retrieve before leaving).

**Hand hygiene:**
- Hand sanitizer (60%+ alcohol) after every WAG bag use
- Bring a small bottle of biodegradable camp soap (Dr. Bronner's) for a real hand wash at camp
- Soap and water > sanitizer. But sanitizer is what you'll actually use at 12,000 ft.

**The math:**
- 3 people × 2 days × 2 WAG bags/person/day = 12 WAG bags
- Weight: ~2 oz per unused bag, ~8–12 oz per used bag
- Total waste kit weight for the team: ~3–4 lbs by the end of the trip
- It's not that bad. It just feels weird the first time.

### What NOT to Do

- ❌ Don't bury waste in snow. It doesn't decompose. It re-emerges when the snow melts. The next climber steps in it.
- ❌ Don't pee on the climbing route. Move 50+ ft off the bootpack.
- ❌ Don't leave TP on the surface. Ever. It doesn't biodegrade at altitude.
- ❌ Don't use cat holes above treeline. The soil is thin/nonexistent and the mountain gets too much traffic.
- ❌ Don't dump pee bottle contents near camp. Walk downhill 50 ft.

### Emotional Reality Check

The first time you use a WAG bag on a mountain, it feels weird and vulnerable. Squatting over a bag in the wind at 10,000 ft while your buddy is 30 feet away melting snow — it's not dignified. That's fine. Everyone on the mountain is doing the same thing. After the first one, it's completely normal. By the third one, you don't think about it.

The alternative — a mountain covered in human waste — is far worse. Pack it out. Leave Shasta clean.
