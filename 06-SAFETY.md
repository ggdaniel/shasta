# Mount Shasta — Safety, Hazards & Emergency

> See also: [Weather](02-WEATHER.md) for go/no-go conditions, [Gear List](03-GEAR-LIST.md) for required safety gear, [Itinerary](07-ITINERARY.md) for turnaround times, and [Ski Descents](10-SKI-DESCENTS.md) for descent-specific hazards.

## Top Hazards (Ranked by Frequency)

### 1. Slip and Fall on Steep Snow/Ice
- **The #1 cause of injuries and rescues on Shasta every year**
- Upper mountain (above Helen Lake) is 30–45° sustained snow
- Pre-dawn: bulletproof ice. One slip = long sliding fall
- **Prevention:** Practice self-arrest BEFORE the trip. Not on the mountain — on a safe slope.
- **Self-arrest drill:** Ice axe technique. You must be able to stop yourself in 2–3 seconds. Practicing head-first, feet-first, face-up, face-down positions.
- [Self-arrest instructional video](https://youtu.be/Sl4zyip2_Ds)
- **Crampons:** Walk with deliberate, flat-footed steps. Avoid catching crampon points on gaiters/pants.

### 2. Weather / Whiteout
- Mountain creates its own weather. Lenticular clouds = storm coming.
- Summit plateau is wide and flat — easy to walk off wrong side in whiteout.
- Happens EVERY year per climbing rangers.
- **Prevention:** Carry GPS with waypoints pre-loaded. Know your bearing from summit back to Red Banks. Turnaround if clouds move in.

### 3. Rockfall & Icefall
- Red Banks area: volcanic rock loosened by freeze-thaw
- Early morning: icefall from melting rime ice
- Mid-morning+: rockfall increases dramatically as sun warms rock
- Rocks travel fast down Avalanche Gulch — hard to dodge
- **Prevention:** Helmet always on above Helen Lake. Pass through Red Banks early (before 7 AM). Don't linger in the gulch.

### 4. Avalanche
- Wind slabs form after storms, even without active snowfall
- Wet loose avalanches on warm afternoons
- **Prevention:** Carry beacon, shovel, probe — [mandatory for ski descent](03-GEAR-LIST.md#%EF%B8%8F-avalanche-safety--required-for-ski-descent). Check [avalanche advisory](02-WEATHER.md#weather-monitoring--check-these-before-and-during-trip) before climbing. Avoid terrain traps.

### 5. Altitude Illness

#### Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS)
| Elevation | Symptoms |
|-----------|----------|
| 8,000–10,000 ft | Mild headache, slight fatigue |
| 10,000–12,000 ft | Headache, nausea, appetite loss, poor sleep |
| 12,000–14,179 ft | Significant fatigue, rest-step required, possible vomiting |

**The golden rule: If symptoms worsen while ascending, DESCEND. Do not push through AMS hoping it improves.**

#### Prevention
- Arrive in Mt Shasta area 1–2 days before climb to acclimatize at ~3,500 ft
- Hydrate aggressively (3–4L per day minimum)
- Eat consistently even when appetite is suppressed
- Ascend slowly — rest-step technique above 10,000 ft
- Consider a practice hike to Horse Camp the day before
- **Medications (discuss with doctor):** Acetazolamide (Diamox) 125mg twice daily starting 1 day before. Ibuprofen 600mg for altitude headache.

#### When to Turn Back
- Persistent vomiting
- Severe headache not relieved by ibuprofen
- Ataxia (can't walk straight — signs of HACE)
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Any team member feels unsafe

### 6. Hypothermia / Frostbite
- Summit temps can be 10°F with wind chill below -20°F
- Wet gloves + wind = rapid heat loss
- **Prevention:** Layering system. Spare gloves. Stay dry. Eat calories. Recognize shivering as first sign.

### 7. Sunburn / Snow Blindness
- UV at 10,000+ ft on snow is extreme
- Snow reflects 80%+ of UV radiation
- **Prevention:** SPF 50+ every 2 hours. Category 4 glacier glasses. Lip balm with SPF. Cover all skin.

## Self-Arrest Protocol

This is the single most important skill for the climb.

1. **Roll onto your stomach** (if you fall on your back)
2. **Ice axe across your chest** — pick-head near shoulder, hand on shaft at hip
3. **Drive the pick into the snow** — arch your body, weight on the axe
4. **Dig toes in** (if NOT wearing crampons) or **keep feet UP** (if wearing crampons — catching a crampon point can flip you)
5. **REACT IMMEDIATELY** — within 2 seconds. Once you're sliding fast, self-arrest becomes nearly impossible on hard snow.

**Practice this at least 10 times on a safe slope before the climb.**

## Team Safety Protocol

1. **Stay together.** No one climbs alone above Helen Lake.
2. **Set turnaround time.** If not at summit by 9 AM, turn back. No exceptions.
3. **Communicate.** Every team member can call for a stop at any time. No judgment.
4. **Share location.** Send a Garmin inReach check-in from Helen Lake and summit.
5. **Leave trip plan.** Share your itinerary with someone not on the trip. Include: route, expected summit day, expected return time, when to call for help if no contact.
