# Mount Shasta — Ski Descent Options: The Complete Guide

Mount Shasta is one of the great ski mountaineering objectives in the lower 48. A massive, freestanding stratovolcano with 7,000+ feet of vertical from multiple aspects — wide-open glacial bowls, steep couloirs, and everything in between. Mid-May is the sweet spot: consolidated snowpack, reliable freeze-thaw corn cycles, long daylight, and coverage all the way to the road.

Here's every viable ski descent option, ranked from most accessible to most committing.

---

## Descent 1: Avalanche Gulch — The Classic ⭐ RECOMMENDED

**The standard. The one everyone skis. And for good reason.**

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Aspect | South-facing |
| Ski vertical | ~5,000 ft (Red Banks to Bunny Flat) or ~7,200 ft if skiing from summit plateau |
| Max pitch | 35–40° at Red Banks chute; 25–30° sustained in the gulch |
| Average pitch | 20–25° (Helen Lake to Horse Camp) |
| Character | Wide-open bowl → narrowing gulch → mellow runout through forest |
| Corn window | 8:00–10:30 AM (sun hits south face early) |
| Approach | Bunny Flat (6,950 ft), skin via Horse Camp |
| Crowds | Moderate. You'll share the descent with other skiers and climbers. |

### The Descent, Section by Section

**Summit Plateau → Top of Red Banks (14,179 ft → 12,400 ft)**
- Can you ski from the actual summit? Sometimes. Depends on snow coverage on the summit plateau and Misery Hill. In a good May, yes — scattered rocks but skiable lines through them.
- More commonly, most skiers bootpack down from the summit to their ski stash at Red Banks and click in there.
- If skiing from the top of Misery Hill: moderate pitch, wide open, wind-affected snow (may be sastrugi or wind crust rather than corn).

**Red Banks Chute (12,400 ft → 12,000 ft)**
- **The crux of the ski descent.** 35–40° for about 400 vertical feet through a rocky chute.
- In mid-May: usually filled in with snow, skiable but steep. Mandatory turns.
- If it's icy or scoured: consider down-climbing in crampons and skiing from below the chute.
- **Hazard:** Rockfall funnels through here. Don't stop in the chute. Ski it decisively, one at a time, with spotters.
- Width: ~50–100 ft depending on snow fill. Not super tight but not wide open either.

**The Gulch: Red Banks → Helen Lake (12,000 ft → 10,443 ft)**
- **This is where it gets good.** The gulch opens up below the Red Banks into a wide, sustained bowl.
- 1,500+ vertical feet of 25–35° consistent pitch.
- In good corn: hero snow. Smooth, predictable, edge-to-edge carving.
- The Heart (rock feature at ~11,200 ft) is visible — ski to either side.
- This section is why people come to Shasta to ski.

**Helen Lake → Horse Camp (10,443 ft → 7,880 ft)**
- Pitch mellows to 15–25°. Wide open snowfield.
- 2,500 vertical feet of mellow cruising.
- Snow gets softer and wetter as you descend and the day warms.
- Late morning: possible mashed-potato snow. Still fun. Fast.
- If you're fast, this section takes 10–15 minutes.

**Horse Camp → Bunny Flat (7,880 ft → 6,950 ft)**
- Forest trail. Mellow grade through trees.
- In mid-May: continuous snow coverage (above-average winters keep this filled in).
- Navigate through trees — some sections require traversing or even a brief skin/pole push if flat.
- In a thin snow year: you might carry skis for the last 0.5 mi.

### Why Choose This Descent
- ✅ Most accessible — standard trailhead, well-known approach
- ✅ South-facing = most reliable corn cycle (sun hits early, softens predictably)
- ✅ Widest margin of error — broad bowl, few obstacles, easy navigation
- ✅ Most beta available — biggest community of skiers reporting conditions
- ✅ Camp at Helen Lake puts you right on the route
- ✅ 5,000 ft of continuous skiing in under an hour

### Downsides
- ❌ Crowded — you'll share with climbers who may be ascending in your fall line
- ❌ South aspect means snow gets cooked by late morning — narrow corn window
- ❌ Red Banks chute is the one technical section that demands respect
- ❌ Rockfall hazard above Helen Lake in late morning

### Skiing Ability Required
Confident intermediate+ backcountry skier. You need to make controlled turns on 35° slopes at 12,000 ft with a pack. If you can ski a black diamond in variable conditions without falling, you can ski Avalanche Gulch. The altitude and the pack are the challenge, not the technical skiing.

## Descent 2: Old Ski Bowl / Green Butte Ridge

**The local's favorite. Slightly off the beaten path. Excellent fall-line skiing.**

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Aspect | South / south-southeast |
| Ski vertical | ~3,500 ft (Old Ski Bowl to Bunny Flat) |
| Max pitch | 30–35° |
| Average pitch | 20–28° |
| Character | Sustained open snowfield with scattered rock features |
| Corn window | 8:00–10:30 AM |
| Approach | Bunny Flat, then diverge east of the standard Avalanche Gulch approach |
| Crowds | Low — most people go straight up the gulch |

### Description
Green Butte Ridge runs along the eastern edge of Avalanche Gulch. The "Old Ski Bowl" was Shasta's original ski area in the 1950s — a wide, south-facing snowfield between Green Butte Ridge and the main gulch. The skiing is sustained, consistent pitch, no funnel hazard, and fewer people.

You can access this as a variation: skin up Avalanche Gulch, then traverse east (skier's left) above Helen Lake and ski the fall line down the Green Butte side. Or approach up the ridge directly from Bunny Flat.

### Why Choose This Descent
- ✅ Less crowded than the main gulch
- ✅ No Red Banks rockfall hazard (you're east of the gulch)
- ✅ Consistent pitch, wide open, excellent fall-line skiing
- ✅ Same trailhead and corn timing as Avalanche Gulch

### Downsides
- ❌ Less vertical than a full summit ski via Avalanche Gulch (~3,500 ft vs ~5,000 ft)
- ❌ Doesn't go to the summit — it's a mid-mountain descent
- ❌ Less defined routing; need to navigate your own line
- ❌ Terrain traps possible if you ski too far into gullies

### Best For
Teams that want to ski laps without going to the summit — skin up 2,000–3,000 ft, ski down, repeat. Or as an alternative descent after summiting if Avalanche Gulch looks too crowded or rockfall-prone.

## Descent 3: Cascade Gulch / West Face

**Steeper, more remote, serious ski mountaineering.**

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Aspect | West / northwest-facing |
| Ski vertical | ~5,500 ft from upper face to treeline |
| Max pitch | 40–50° (upper face) |
| Average pitch | 30–35° |
| Character | Sustained steep face with couloir options |
| Corn window | 10:00 AM–1:00 PM (west aspect — sun hits later) |
| Approach | Typically accessed by ascending Avalanche Gulch then traversing west, or from Hidden Valley approach |
| Crowds | Very low — you may have it to yourself |

### Description
The west face of Shasta is a different mountain from the south side. Steeper sustained angle, less skied, more consequential. Cascade Gulch is a broad couloir/face system on the western flank that drops ~5,500 vertical feet from the summit area to treeline.

The key advantage for skiing: west-facing aspect means the sun hits later in the day. If the south-facing corn window has closed (too soft by 10 AM), the west face is just coming into corn. This gives you more scheduling flexibility.

### Why Choose This Descent
- ✅ Steeper, more committing — a step up in ski mountaineering
- ✅ Later corn window gives more time flexibility
- ✅ Very few people — real solitude
- ✅ Impressive vertical if you can ski from near the summit

### Downsides
- ❌ 40–50° sustained pitches — expert skiing only
- ❌ Higher avalanche risk (steeper, later warming)
- ❌ Complex approach — either traverse from standard route or use a less-established approach trail
- ❌ Less information/beta available
- ❌ Harder to bail — you're committing to a different drainage

### Skiing Ability Required
Expert. Confident on 45° slopes with a pack at altitude. If you need to think about whether you can ski this, you can't. This is steep Cascades volcano skiing, not groomed blacks.

## Descent 4: Hotlum-Bolam / North Side

**The big-mountain line. Remote, glaciated, the real alpine experience.**

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Aspect | North / northeast-facing |
| Ski vertical | ~6,000–7,000 ft from summit to treeline |
| Max pitch | 35–45° (Hotlum headwall) |
| Average pitch | 25–35° |
| Character | Glacier skiing, crevasse navigation, wide-open north face |
| Corn window | 11:00 AM–2:00 PM (north aspect — sun hits latest) |
| Approach | North Gate or Bolam trailhead — longer, more remote |
| Crowds | Minimal — you'll likely be alone |

### Description
The Hotlum and Bolam glaciers on Shasta's north side offer the longest, most sustained ski descents on the mountain. The Hotlum Glacier is the largest glacier in California and the skiing reflects it — massive scale, real glacier travel, and a descent that feels more like a big Alaska line than a Cascades volcano.

You ascend the Hotlum-Bolam Ridge (between the two glaciers), summit, then ski down either the Hotlum Glacier (steeper, longer) or the Bolam Glacier (slightly mellower but still massive).

The north aspect means snow stays colder and firmer longer. The corn window comes later in the day, which can be an advantage or a disadvantage depending on your schedule.

### Why Choose This Descent
- ✅ The most vertical — potential for 7,000 ft of skiing
- ✅ True glacier skiing — Alaska-scale feel in the lower 48
- ✅ Solitude — you might see no one all day
- ✅ North-facing snow quality often superior (less sun-damaged, more consistent)
- ✅ If you've already skied Avalanche Gulch and want more

### Downsides
- ❌ Crevasse hazard — rope team, glacier travel skills, and crevasse rescue competency required
- ❌ Longer approach — 2+ hour drive to trailhead, longer skin approach
- ❌ Trailhead access uncertain in May (North Gate road may be snowed in)
- ❌ Complex navigation — north side has fewer landmarks, weather moves in faster
- ❌ Harder rescue — much more remote, SAR response time is longer
- ❌ Corn window starts later — may not fully soften until midday, increasing exposure time

### Skiing Ability Required
Advanced to expert. Must be competent in glacier travel, crevasse rescue, rope management. The skiing itself is in the advanced-intermediate range (wide open, sustained 30–35°) but the environment adds significant commitment.

- **Shasta Mountaineering School — Hotlum-Bolam conditions report:** https://climbshasta.com/2024/07/03/conditions-report-july-1-2024-climbing-mt-shastas-hotlum-bolam-ridge/ (includes photos of the ridge and glacier)

## Descent 5: Whitney Glacier

**The expert line. Shasta's largest glacier. Steep, crevassed, committing.**

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Aspect | Northwest |
| Ski vertical | ~5,000–6,000 ft |
| Max pitch | 40–50° (headwall) |
| Average pitch | 30–40° |
| Character | Active glacier, icefall, crevasses, steep headwall |
| Approach | Whitney Creek trailhead or traverse from west side |
| Crowds | Nearly zero |

### Description
Whitney Glacier is the longest glacier in California and one of the most committing ski descents on the mountain. The upper headwall is 40–50° and funnels into a crevassed glacier below. This is real alpine terrain — icefall, seracs, and route-finding through crevasse fields.

This is not a descent for your first time on Shasta. It's the kind of line you come back for after doing Avalanche Gulch and Hotlum-Bolam and wanting something harder.

### Why / Why Not
- ✅ Possibly the most impressive ski descent in the Cascades
- ✅ Complete solitude
- ✅ True alpine glacier skiing
- ❌ Extreme crevasse hazard — rope mandatory, not optional
- ❌ Icefall sections may be impassable some seasons
- ❌ Very complex approach
- ❌ High objective hazard (serac fall, icefall)
- ❌ Almost no contemporary trip reports — you're largely on your own

## Recommendation for May 17

**Avalanche Gulch. Definitively.** For a first ski descent on Shasta with a 3-person team:

1. It's the most accessible, best-documented, widest-margin-of-error descent
2. Mid-May corn cycle is dialed on the south face
3. You don't need glacier gear
4. The Red Banks chute is exciting but manageable (35–40° for 400 ft)
5. 5,000 ft of continuous skiing to the car

If conditions are perfect and you have extra energy on Day 1, consider skinning up to the Old Ski Bowl area for a mellow warm-up lap (2,000 vert) before setting up camp at Helen Lake.

Save the north side and west face for the return trip. You'll want to come back.

### Google Earth Recon
Open Google Earth, go to Mount Shasta (41.409, -122.195), and switch to 3D view. Orbit the mountain slowly. You can visually trace every descent:
- South face (Avalanche Gulch) — the wide, obvious bowl
- West face (Cascade Gulch) — the steeper face to the right
- North face (Hotlum/Bolam glaciers) — the massive north-side glacier system
- The summit plateau — where bootpacking and skiing decisions are made

This 5-minute Google Earth session is worth more than any written description for understanding the mountain's geometry.
